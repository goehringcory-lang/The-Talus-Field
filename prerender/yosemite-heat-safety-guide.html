There's a version of Yosemite that exists in the collective imagination where the weather is always perfect. Golden light, cool breezes, maybe a dramatic cloud or two for the photographs. I've been working as a naturalist in this park for close to two decades, and I can tell you that version exists for about six weeks a year, split between May and October. The rest of the summer, Yosemite Valley is hot. Not Phoenix hot, not Death Valley hot, but a specific kind of hot that catches people off guard precisely because they didn't plan for it.

Yosemite Valley sits at roughly 4,000 feet in elevation, which sounds like it should be cool. It isn't. The Valley is a granite box, roughly seven miles long and a mile wide, with walls that rise 3,000 to 4,000 feet on either side. In July and August, daytime highs reach 90 degrees Fahrenheit regularly and occasionally crack 100. The granite absorbs heat all morning, radiates it all afternoon, and doesn't let go until well after dark. There is no sea breeze. There is no lake effect. There is no relief until the sun drops behind the south rim somewhere around 7 PM. Heat is the thing that catches more visitors off guard than anything else, with the possible exception of the altitude. People pack for postcard Yosemite. They should pack for a stone oven. If you're planning your first trip, this is the piece most planning guides leave out.

How hot Yosemite actually gets in summer

How hot is Yosemite in summer? The monthly averages tell part of the story.

MonthYosemite Valley (4,000 ft)Tuolumne Meadows (8,600 ft)
June~82°F~62°F
July~90°F~72°F
August~90°F~70°F
September~83°F~65°F

Those numbers are measured in the shade, at a weather station, which is not where you'll be standing on the Mist Trail staircase at 1 PM. Yosemite summer temperatures are average highs, meaning half the days are hotter.

The averages are misleading in another way, too. Valley floor granite surfaces can hit 140 to 160 degrees Fahrenheit in direct sun. Dark granite, the kind that makes up large sections of the north wall, runs 30 degrees hotter than light granite. When you're hiking a south-facing trail in July, you're not just dealing with air temperature. You're dealing with radiant heat bouncing off every surface around you. Granite walls reflect and radiate heat from three directions. The trail surface cooks from below. And there's nothing to cut it. No wind off a body of water, no altitude-driven cooling, nothing. One hundred degree days happen roughly once a year, usually in late July, but the days that hover at 95 and feel like 100 on exposed granite are the ones that actually fill up the clinic.

Exposed trails that will cook you

If you're hiking Yosemite in summer, not all trails are equal in the heat, and the differences matter more than most people realize. Some trails that are perfectly reasonable in May become genuinely dangerous in July.

Four Mile Trail, the steep route from the Valley floor to Glacier Point, is the one the National Park Service has specifically called "very dangerous, exposed." It faces south. Long stretches have no shade whatsoever. It gains 3,200 feet in less than five miles. On a 90-degree day, the combination of sustained climbing, direct sun, and radiant heat off the granite makes this trail a heat casualty machine. I have personally helped evacuate people from this trail. Every time, they started too late.

Upper Yosemite Falls trail is the other big one. The first mile, up to Columbia Rock, has some shade and is manageable early in the day. Above Columbia Rock, the switchbacks become fully exposed. You're climbing granite slabs with the Valley wall reflecting heat directly onto you. It's relentless, and there's no water source on the trail.

The Mist Trail above Vernal Fall footbridge is deceptive. Below the footbridge, you get the famous mist from the waterfall, which feels incredible on a hot day. Above it, you get a granite staircase in direct sun. The mist is gone. The shade is gone. You're climbing 600 stone steps with nothing between you and the sky. People don't expect the transition because the lower section felt so refreshing.

Half Dome's subdome and cables sit at 8,800 feet, which should be cooler, and the air temperature is. But the sun at that elevation is unrelenting, the granite reflects it from every angle, and there is nowhere to stop in shade on the subdome or the cables. You're completely committed once you start. Add the physical intensity of the cable climb and the fact that most people have already hiked 8 miles to get there, and you have a serious heat exposure problem even at elevation.

The Panorama Trail, an 8-mile descent from Glacier Point to the Valley, is the one people underestimate most. It sounds easy because it's "all downhill." It's 8 miles of exposed granite with limited shade, and the descent hammers your knees, which makes you drink more water, which you may not have brought enough of.

Here's something most visitors don't know: the heat actually causes rockfalls. A USGS study found that roughly 15 percent of unexplained rockfalls in Yosemite occur between noon and 6 PM from July through September. Thermal expansion of the granite. The rock quite literally swells in the heat and pieces break away. So the trails aren't just hot. They're dropping rocks.

The best strategy is simple and inflexible. The best time to hike Yosemite in summer is before 7 AM. Be done with exposed sections by 10 or 11 in the morning. I know that's early. I know you're on vacation. The window is real, and it doesn't negotiate. Check the best hikes guide for trails that work well with an early start.

The water math

The NPS guidance says to carry one quart of water per hour of hiking in the heat. That's the official number, and it's fine as a baseline. My real guidance, from someone who has worked here through every July since the mid-2000s, is to carry more than you think you need. A full day on an exposed trail in July means 4 to 6 liters per person. That's heavy. A liter of water weighs 2.2 pounds. Six liters is over 13 pounds of water alone. You carry it anyway.

There's a counterbalancing danger that doesn't get enough attention. Hyponatremia, low blood sodium, happens when you drink too much plain water without replacing electrolytes. Your body dilutes its own sodium levels. The symptoms look a lot like heat exhaustion: nausea, headache, confusion. Which means people sometimes respond by drinking more water, which makes it worse. Carry electrolyte tablets or salty snacks. Trail mix exists for a reason. Pretzels are genuinely good trail food in the heat.

You should know the difference between heat exhaustion and heat stroke because one is a bad afternoon and the other is a medical emergency. Heat exhaustion means heavy sweating, nausea, dizziness, pale and clammy skin. You feel terrible. You get into shade, you rest, you drink water with electrolytes, and you recover. Heat stroke means sweating stops. Skin goes red and dry. Confusion sets in, sometimes aggressiveness or slurred speech. This is a 911 call. This is a person whose body has lost the ability to cool itself. Cool them aggressively. Wet clothing, shade, fanning. Get medical help immediately. By some estimates, roughly a quarter of nontraumatic search and rescue missions in Yosemite are dehydration or heat related. That's nearly one in four. You don't want to be in that statistic.

What to wear on the trail

A quick note on clothing, because what you wear matters as much as what you carry. Light-colored, loose-fitting clothing reflects heat better than dark clothes. A wide-brim hat is not optional. UPF-rated sun hoodies have become standard gear for anyone who works outside in the Sierra, and there's a reason for that: they cover more skin than sunscreen and don't wear off. Speaking of sunscreen, reapply every two hours, and know that the UV at 4,000 feet is already stronger than at sea level, and at 8,000 feet it's meaningfully more intense. The granite reflects UV from below, the thin air filters less of it from above, and the combination will burn you faster than you expect. A bandana or cooling towel soaked in cold creek water and draped around your neck is the single cheapest piece of heat management gear you can carry.

The escape uphill

The single best heat strategy in Yosemite is also the simplest: go higher. Temperature drops roughly 3.5 degrees Fahrenheit per thousand feet of elevation gain. The math works in your favor quickly.

Tuolumne Meadows sits at 8,600 feet and runs 18 to 20 degrees cooler than the Valley. When it's 90 in Yosemite Valley, Tuolumne is 72. The hiking is better than anything in the Valley, the crowds are a fraction, and the temperature is the kind of summer weather you actually imagined when you booked the trip. Glacier Point at 7,200 feet is meaningfully cooler than the Valley floor, and the views compete with anything you'll see from below. Crane Flat and White Wolf are intermediate options if you want to get out of the Valley heat without driving all the way to Tuolumne.

Tioga Road is the heat escape that matters most. The corridor from Crane Flat at 6,200 feet to Tioga Pass at 9,945 feet drops temperatures dramatically. You can leave the Valley at 95 degrees and be in a light jacket at Olmsted Point an hour later. It's open roughly late May through early November, depending on snow, and in July and August there is no reason not to use it if the heat is getting to you.

Tenaya Lake, at 8,150 feet along Tioga Road, deserves special mention. Cold water, a sandy beach on the east end, and a 20-degree temperature drop from the Valley. It's one of the best places in the park on a hot day. More on swimming spots below.

Yosemite thunderstorms and lightning safety

Here's where the heat escape gets complicated. The high country that saves you from the heat also has the most dangerous weather in the park.

Sierra Nevada afternoon thunderstorms are one of the most predictable weather patterns in the American West. The mechanism is straightforward. Summer sun heats the granite, the granite heats the air, warm air rises, hits moisture at altitude, and builds into thunderheads. In July and August, you can watch this happen like clockwork. By 11 AM it's common to see cumulus clouds building over the peaks. By early afternoon, those clouds have gone vertical and dark. By 1 PM, lightning.

The rule is simple. Be off exposed ridges, peaks, and open granite by noon. I don't say this as conservative advice. I don't say it as a suggestion. This is the rule. I've watched lightning strike Cathedral Peak from Tuolumne Meadows on a day that started blue and cloudless. The buildup from "nice morning" to "violent electrical storm" can happen in under two hours.

Specific locations where you absolutely do not want to be when lightning starts: Half Dome cables, where you are standing on an exposed granite dome holding a metal cable. Cathedral Peak. Lembert Dome. Any high-country pass above treeline. The summit of Clouds Rest. If you are above treeline and you hear thunder, you are already within strike range.

Lightning safety is not complicated. Get below treeline. Get off water. Get low, not flat, but crouching with your feet together. Don't shelter under a lone tree; it's a lightning magnet. Don't huddle in a group. Spread out so a single strike can't hit everyone. The monsoon pattern typically runs from mid-July through September, with the most intense activity in late July and August. Where to swim in Yosemite

Swimming is the best thing about Yosemite in summer that most first-time visitors don't plan for. They come with hiking boots and trail maps. They should also bring a swimsuit and a towel.

The realistic swimming season runs from about mid-July through mid-September. Before that, the Merced River is running too high, too fast, and too cold with Sierra snowmelt. This point is non-negotiable: do not swim in May or June. The river in spring is a powerful, cold, silty current that does not care how strong a swimmer you are. Most river fatalities in Yosemite occur during the high-water period. The water looks inviting. It isn't.

By mid to late July, the river drops to swimmable levels and the water warms enough that getting in is a pleasure rather than a shock.

In the Valley, Sentinel Beach is the most accessible swimming spot. Sandy bank, a picnic area right there, views of Half Dome from the water. It's family-friendly and usually calm by late summer. Cathedral Beach, just downstream, is wider and deeper, with El Capitan framed in the view upstream. Watch the downstream current here, especially earlier in the season. The Housekeeping Camp area has a wider, slower section of river behind Curry Village. Raft rentals are available at Curry Village for the float from Stoneman Bridge to Sentinel Beach, a mellow three-mile drift that may be the best way to experience the Valley on a 90-degree day. Swinging Bridge offers shallow sandy bottoms that are good for wading and casual cooling off, especially with kids.

Outside the Valley, Tenaya Lake is the best subalpine swimming in the park. Sandy beach, clear water, mountain backdrop. The water temperature runs 55 to 60 degrees, which is bracing but tolerable once you commit. Wawona, at lower elevation with a smaller drainage, has the warmest swimming in the park.

The safety rules for swimming in Yosemite are short and absolute. Never swim above a waterfall. Ever. The current accelerates toward the lip in ways you cannot feel until it's too late, and people die this way. Don't swim in May or June. Cold water shock is real; water at 50 to 60 degrees triggers an immediate involuntary gasp reflex on immersion, and the shock response can impair muscle control for up to 90 seconds. Don't swim alone in deep cold water.

Shady trails along the river

On a hot day, the best hike in Yosemite might be no hike at all. Or rather, the best hike might be a flat, shaded walk along the river where the goal is not a destination but just being outside without suffering.

Sections of the Valley Loop Trail along the Merced River are shaded by black oaks, cottonwoods, and the Valley's dense riparian corridor. The temperature under that canopy can be 10 to 15 degrees cooler than the exposed meadows 200 yards away. The Mirror Lake and Tenaya Creek trail is one of the shadiest walks in the Valley, a flat meander through forest along a creek bed. In late summer, Mirror Lake itself is mostly a sandy flat, but the walk to get there is cool and quiet. The path from Swinging Bridge to Sentinel Beach along the south bank of the Merced is another good one, flat and shaded and never far from water you can wade into.

The Lower Yosemite Fall loop is short, partially shaded, and runs close to Yosemite Creek. In July the waterfall itself is usually reduced to a trickle or gone entirely, but the walk is still pleasant and the creek still has some flow.

The key insight, and this is something it took me years of watching overheated, miserable visitors to fully appreciate, is that the park is not going anywhere. You don't have to summit something every day. On a 95-degree afternoon, a walk along the river in the shade, a swim, a nap under a black oak while staring up at granite walls, that is not wasting your time in Yosemite. That is Yosemite. The wildlife guide has more on what you might spot along the river corridor in summer, mule deer in the meadows at dusk, dippers working the riffles, the occasional coyote. The shade trails are where the animals are, too, because they're not stupid about heat either.

Your car is also a problem

One more thing that people don't think about until they're living it. Your car, parked in a trailhead lot in full sun for six hours while you hike, will be nearly uninhabitable when you return. Car interiors can reach 130 degrees within an hour on a 90-degree day, and climb past 140 within a couple of hours. The steering wheel will burn your hands. The seatbelt buckle will brand your skin.

Don't leave pets in the car. Don't leave kids in the car. Don't leave medications in the car. Most medications degrade at those temperatures, including insulin, inhalers, and epinephrine. Trailhead parking lots in Yosemite are almost universally fully exposed. There are no covered parking structures. There are very few shaded spots, and they fill first.

Leave a towel draped over the steering wheel. Leave windows cracked if you feel safe doing so. Bring a second shirt to change into, because the dry one will feel transformative after a sweaty hike. And carry an extra liter of water in the car for the drive out. You will be thirstier than you expect.

The takeaway

The heat in Yosemite is manageable with planning and dangerous without it. That's the whole thing. It's not complicated. Start early. Carry more water than you think you need. Know which trails are exposed and plan your day around that exposure. Know the difference between heat exhaustion and heat stroke. Go uphill when the Valley is a furnace. Watch the sky when you do.

And consider that some of the best summer days in this park have nothing to do with trails at all. A riverside afternoon in the shade. A cold swim at Cathedral Beach with El Capitan turning gold above you. A drive up to Tenaya Lake where it's 70 degrees and the water is clear and the granite slabs are warm enough to lie on but not hot enough to burn. That's Yosemite in the heat, done right. Plan for it and you'll have a story about the best swim of your life. Don't plan for it and you might end up in the back of an ambulance wondering what happened. I've seen both outcomes more times than I can count.
