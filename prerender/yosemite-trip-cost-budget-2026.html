People ask me what a Yosemite trip costs and I always give the same answer: it depends entirely on how you want to do it. Which is true but unhelpful. So here's the actually helpful version.

I've worked in this park for years. I've watched people do Yosemite for $50 a day and I've watched people do it for $800 a day. Both groups had great trips. The difference isn't quality. It's choices. And the choices are more straightforward than the internet makes them seem.

Let me walk through every cost category, give you real 2026 numbers, and then show you what three very different Yosemite trips actually add up to.

Entrance fees

The big one that everyone knows about: $35 per vehicle for a seven-day pass. That's per car, not per person. A family of five pays the same $35 as a solo traveler.

If you're visiting more than one national park this year, or if you'll visit Yosemite more than twice, buy the America the Beautiful annual pass for $80. It covers every national park and federal recreation area in the country for a full year. One of the best deals in the federal government.

If you're entering on foot, bicycle, or horse, the fee is $20 per person. Motorcycles are $30 (valid for up to two motorcycles and four people).

One thing that simplifies planning: Yosemite is not requiring timed-entry reservations in 2026. In previous years, a reservation was needed to drive in during peak hours. That system is not in effect this year, so the entrance fee is all you pay to get in. Check the current status before your trip, since the policy can change year to year.

Lodging: the biggest variable

This is where trips diverge. Here's the real range:

Camping (cheapest): $24–$36 per night for a campsite. Valley campgrounds (Upper Pines, Lower Pines, North Pines) are $36. The more remote Tioga Road campgrounds (Tamarack Flat, Yosemite Creek, Porcupine Flat) are $24. Camp 4, the walk-in Valley campground, is $10 per person per night. For a couple, that's $20.

In-park cabins and tent cabins: Half Dome Village tent cabins run $170–$200 per night. You're getting a canvas roof and a cot, but you're sleeping in Yosemite Valley. Hard-walled cabins with bathrooms are $220–$300.

In-park lodges: Yosemite Valley Lodge rooms run $300–$500 per night depending on room type and season (summer rates are highest). The Ahwahnee starts around $580 for a standard room and climbs past $1,400 for suites. (The Wawona Hotel, normally $200–$300, is closed indefinitely for repairs as of 2026.)

Gateway towns: Motels and hotels in Mariposa, Oakhurst, Groveland, and El Portal range from $120 to $300 per night depending on the town and the season. Vacation rentals (Airbnb, VRBO) can be a deal for groups. A cabin that sleeps six for $250 per night is under $42 per person.

The lodging decision is covered in detail in my guide to the gateway towns, but for budgeting purposes: camping saves you hundreds of dollars, and gateway towns save you a hundred or more compared to in-park hotels.

Food: bring your own or eat in the park

Park food is fine but expensive. Here's what you're looking at:

Casual dining in the park: A deli sandwich and a drink at Degnan's or the Valley Lodge cafeteria runs $18–$25 per person. Pizza from the Pizza Deck at Half Dome Village is $27–$35 for a pie (medium to large). A decent sit-down dinner at the Mountain Room at Yosemite Valley Lodge is $35–$55 per person.

The Ahwahnee Dining Room: Breakfast is $25–$35 (the buffet runs about $32). As of April 2026, dinner is prix fixe only: $95 for five courses or $125 for seven courses ($135/$165 with wine pairings). Reservations are required; walk-ins are no longer accepted. It's a memorable experience and worth doing once if the budget allows, but it's not an everyday meal.

Camp cooking: If you're camping or staying somewhere with a kitchen, bringing your own food is the single biggest money-saver on a Yosemite trip. A cooler stocked at a Mariposa or Oakhurst grocery store before you enter the park will cost $15–$25 per person per day for three meals plus snacks. I've seen families of four eat well for a full week on $400 of groceries.

The Village Store: There's a general store in Yosemite Village that sells groceries, but prices are 30–50% higher than a regular supermarket. Use it for things you forgot, not as your primary shopping.

My advice: bring a cooler, stock it before you arrive, and plan to eat one meal per day at a park restaurant if you want the experience. Trail lunches of sandwiches, fruit, and trail mix are better than anything you'll buy in the park anyway.

Gas and driving

The drive to Yosemite is not trivial, and gas costs add up.

From San Francisco / Bay Area: About 170 miles each way. At current gas prices (~$4.75/gallon in California) and average fuel economy (28 mpg), that's roughly $25–$30 each way, or $55–$65 round trip. If you're making a day trip from the city, that's your floor just for fuel.

From Los Angeles: About 280 miles each way. Figure $45–$50 each way, $90–$100 round trip.

From Sacramento / Central Valley: About 170 miles, $25–$30 each way.

Once you're in the park: Gas is available at Wawona and at Crane Flat (both have 24-hour pay-at-the-pump with credit card). There's also a station in El Portal, just outside the Arch Rock entrance. All are expensive, typically $0.50 to $1.00 more per gallon than in gateway towns like Mariposa or Oakhurst. Fill up before you enter. The driving and parking logistics inside the park are their own adventure, but you won't burn much gas on the Valley shuttle loop.

Don't forget parking: Parking in Yosemite is free but competitive. There's no paid parking anywhere in the park. The cost isn't money. It's time.

Gear: what you actually need

The outdoor industry wants you to believe you need $2,000 of gear to visit a national park. You don't.

Shoes: Sturdy, broken-in shoes with good tread. If you own hiking boots, great. If you don't, a pair of trail runners ($80–$150) will handle every trail in Yosemite except Half Dome. Running shoes and fashion sneakers are genuinely not safe on the steep, wet granite of the park's most popular hikes. This is the one gear purchase I'd say is non-negotiable.

Layers: Yosemite's temperature swings are dramatic. A summer day can go from 40°F at dawn to 95°F by noon to 55°F after sunset. Bring a warm layer (fleece or puffy jacket) and a rain layer even in summer. You probably already own these.

Day pack: Something to carry water, snacks, sunscreen, and a layer. A school backpack works. If you want to buy one, $30–$60 gets a perfectly good pack.

Water bottles: Bring them. The park has water refill stations. Don't buy single-use plastic bottles in the park. It's wasteful and expensive.

What you don't need: Trekking poles (unless you're doing Half Dome or have knee issues), a GPS device (your phone works fine), bear spray (prohibited in Yosemite; the bears here are black bears, not grizzlies), specialized hiking pants, or a $200 headlamp.

Total gear cost for someone starting from scratch: $100–$200 if you're buying shoes and a pack. $0 if you already own reasonable outdoor clothing and a backpack.

Guided programs and activities

Yosemite Conservancy runs guided programs in the park, including naturalist walks, stargazing programs, and custom adventures, that range from $15 to several hundred dollars depending on the program type and duration.

Free programs: Ranger talks and evening programs are completely free. Yosemite Conservancy naturalist walks and stargazing programs are affordable: sunset walks are $15 per person and stargazing programs are $25 per person. The stargazing programs are some of the best things happening in the park.

Paid guided programs: Yosemite Conservancy custom adventures start at $500 for a half-day (up to four people) and $850 for a full day, with $50 per additional person. Multi-day and specialty programs cost more.

For budgeting, guided programs are optional but genuinely good. If you're a first-time visitor and can fit one into the budget, a guided program with a naturalist who knows the park will change what you see and understand about Yosemite.

The three budget tiers

Here's what three different four-day, three-night trips look like for two people.

Shoestring: ~$400–$550 total ($200–$275 per person)

  • Lodging: Campground, $36/night x 3 = $108
  • Food: Cooler of groceries from home or a gateway-town grocery stop, ~$25/person/day x 3 days = $150
  • Gas: Round trip from Bay Area, ~$60
  • Entrance fee: $35
  • Gear: $0 (using what you own)
  • Dining out: One meal at a park restaurant, ~$50
  • Total: ~$403

This is a real trip. A great trip. You eat well, you see everything, and you wake up in the park. Camping in Yosemite is not a compromise. It's the best way to be here.

Comfortable mid-range: ~$1,400–$1,800 total ($700–$900 per person)

  • Lodging: Yosemite Valley Lodge or gateway-town hotel, $350/night x 3 = $1,050
  • Food: Mix of camp-style lunches and park dining, ~$50/person/day x 3 = $300
  • Gas: Round trip from Bay Area, ~$60
  • Entrance fee: $35
  • Gear: Trail shoes if needed, $80
  • Guided program: One stargazing program, ~$50 for two
  • Total: ~$1,575

This is the trip most people end up doing. Private room, some good meals, a mix of self-guided and guided activities. It's comfortable without being extravagant, and you don't feel like you're pinching pennies the whole time.

Splurge: ~$3,500–$5,000+ total ($1,750–$2,500 per person)

  • Lodging: The Ahwahnee, $650/night x 3 = $1,950
  • Food: Ahwahnee dining room dinners (prix fixe from $95/person) plus nice park lunches, ~$150/person/day x 3 = $900
  • Gas: Round trip from Bay Area, ~$60
  • Entrance fee: $35
  • Guided program: Full-day custom adventure, ~$850 for two
  • Extras: Souvenirs, specialty gear, etc. ~$200
  • Total: ~$3,995

This is the trip where everything is dialed. You're sleeping in a historic lodge, eating well, and getting the VIP version of the park. It's a special-occasion trip and it's worth every dollar for the right celebration.

Hidden costs people forget

Bear canisters: If you're backpacking, you need a bear canister. Rent one in the park for $5/week from any staffed wilderness permit station (plus a $95 refundable deposit). Car campers use the bear lockers at every campsite (free).

Tips: Guided programs and sit-down dining. Budget 18–20% on top of listed prices.

The gift shop purchase: Everyone says they won't. Everyone does. Budget $20 for the mug or the patch or the sticker.

The bottom line

Yosemite can be done cheaply. That's the thing that surprises people most. The park itself doesn't cost much. Thirty-five dollars to get in, free shuttles, free trails, free ranger programs. The expensive part is lodging, and even that is manageable if you camp or stay in a gateway town.

Don't let the cost keep you from going. The people having the best time in this park are not always the ones spending the most money. They're the ones who showed up, planned well, and spent their time outside instead of in a hotel room. If you're starting from scratch, my first-timer's guide to Yosemite ties it all together.
